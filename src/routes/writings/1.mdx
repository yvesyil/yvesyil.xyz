## Life in Pictures

After having the "writings" section in my current website for a while now, I decided that I should really start writing down my thoughts/experiences. I recently bought my first camera, a [Fujifilm X-T50](https://www.fujifilm-x.com/global/products/cameras/x-t50/), and first lens, [Fujinon XF18-55mm f/2.8-4.0](https://www.fujifilm-x.com/global/products/lenses/xf18-55mmf28-4-r-lm-ois/).

I always liked the feeling that I get when I'm able to capture a good picture with my phone camera. But apart from knowing simple tricks like, not taking a photo of someone if the sun is behind them, my knowledge was pretty much zero. I gave up pretty quickly if the lightning conditions were not up to par relative to the angle that I was trying to take the photo. I did not try to move or try to create a different angle that would work with the light, I let constraints get in the way of my creativity.

Midway through high school, I started watching films directed by Stanley Kubrick. Even to my untrained eye, I could tell there was something special about the way Stanley Kubrick told stories through the lens. Almost every scene in his films looked like a painting, deliberately framed to create an aesthetically pleasing image.

What sparked my interest in photography didn't happen until two years ago. I was watching [Stanley Kubrick: A Life in Pictures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stanley_Kubrick:_A_Life_in_Pictures), when it suddenly awakened a strong sense of awe the moment I saw this photo:

![Photo by Stanley Kubrick](/img/w1/stanley_kubrick1.jpg)

This had to be one of the best photo's I had ever seen. The sadness on the clerk's face is undeniably real and it immediately tells us the news carries sorrow.
The raw sincerity of the emotion captured in this photograph was exactly what I dreamed of emulating in my own art. It made me realize that photography was far more profound than I had imagined, and I felt compelled to take part in it myself.

> "I am not interested in shooting new things - I am interested to see things new." <br></br> — Ernst Haas

For the average folk, shooting with a phone camera is more than sufficient. Taking a photo requires very little effort: you simply frame the scene and press a button, and you instantly get a decent result. Exposure, focus, and other settings are handled automatically for you.
The default camera app on a phone is designed for maximum simplicity. Because of this, the gap between seeing an image with your eyes and actually capturing it is almost nonexistent.



Of course, this simplicity comes at a cost, the sensor inside a phone camera is tiny compared to the sensor in a dedicated camera. This is a big deal in low-light conditions (this doesn't necessarily mean taking a photo in a dark place, I will explain this later). To compensate the small size of their sensors, phones usually do extensive post-processing in the background. Although this does improve the end result compared to not doing anything, it's still inferior to the quality you can achieve just by having a bigger sensor. Another limitation is the default camera app itself. Unless you've purchesed an app with "pro" photography capabilities, the default app usually doesn't even allow you to take control over the camera's most fundamental parameters. There’s also the experience of using a real camera. A phone prioritizes convenience and portability, but in doing so, it strips away much of the tactile feeling and intentionality that come with shooting on a dedicated camera.

So, I decided to get a dedicated camera. Before making the purchase, I dug deep into the photography rabbit-hole, the first thing I looked up was how a digital camera captures images. This lead to learning about exposure and different parameters that affect it. It turns out if you understand these three parameters, then you've already learned the required knowledge to take your first picture:

- [Shutter speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shutter_speed) is the amount of time that the sensor is exposed to the light via the shutter. If you need to capture a fast moving object, your shutter speed needs to be fast at the expense of less light hitting the sensor.
- [Aperture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aperture) is the pupil of the camera lens. The larger the pupil the larger light captured at cost of depth-of-field.
- [ISO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Film_speed#ISO) is the light sensitivity of the sensor. A high ISO number amplifies the amount of light entering through the lens. But it's good to remember that any kind of amplification introduces noise.

**Light is the primary resource in photography**. Aside from the light already present in a scene, every camera setting you adjust involves a trade-off. Photography is often about balancing these settings to achieve the look you want while maintaining proper exposure. If you want a cleaner image with less noise, you lower the ISO. However, lowering the ISO also reduces the amount of light captured by the sensor, so you compensate by either widening the aperture, or decreasing the shutter speed. Suppose you want to keep more of the image in focus, so you choose not to widen the aperture. Instead, you lower the shutter speed. This allows more light into the camera, but it also introduces motion blur. Depending on the kind of image you are trying to create, that blur can either be a problem or a deliberate artistic effect:

In the beginning, it took me a while to get used to thinking about exposure in terms of these three variables. But as I took more photos in different lighting conditions, I gradually became better at estimating and balancing them. Once I learned I enjoyed taking pictures far more than I ever had before.

What I realized is that the more effort you willingly put into something, the more value the end result tends to have, both in your own eyes and, often, in the eyes of others. In an era defined by instant access and overconsumption, I find myself wanting to do the opposite: to slow down, take time, and put genuine effort into creating work that feels meaningful, at least to me.
